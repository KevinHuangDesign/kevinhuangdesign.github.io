overview
Current options for academic support is seen as inconvenient and unreliable, discouraging already busy college students from seeking help. Students feel the need to get help, often when it's too late. Instead of procrastinating on your phone, what if the solution was that habitual?

Recognizing a gap between my friends who need help and tutors who know the ropes, I developed an app prototype that connects students to peer tutors for help in seconds. It's like Uber, but for ensuring every student gets support tailored to their needs.
challenge

What are we trying to solve?

How might we help college students get effective help while also balancing convenience and price?
project scope

Narrowing it down

As learning support can be a broad topic in which many aspects need to be accounted for, this project is focused on addressing the barriers to seeking help. Specifically, focusing on how a student can find help in the most efficient way while allowing them to study on their own time.

“Differences across professors is the hardest part in helping students. Sometimes none of us there have had that professor so we’re not really sure how to answer.”

“I was always too busy to attend review sessions so I usually study with my friends, but Zoom makes it awkward. The one time I did go right before midterms, I waited 30 minutes just to ask one question.” 

“Last semester I was so stressed studying for Finance. I wish I had somebody who could help because my friends weren’t able to.”

How I approached the problem

What if you could just take a photo of what your problem and someone comes to the rescue?

It's crucial to understand the unique ecosystem before brainstorming solutions. Nearly all USC students live within 2 miles of campus and get free rides, affordding effortless study sessions with friends. But with social distancing in effect, this is no longer possible. In addition, the USC Marshall School of Business has a competitive grading curve that paradoxically deters students from collaborating with classmates.

I wanted to resuscitate collaborative interactions through instantly closing the gap between students who need help and tutors who know the ropes.

Designing behavior

Existing options and competitors for academic support fail to be viable because students only realize they need help right before the exam. So how can I change the experience of seeking help from being ineffective to fulfilling?

I researched habit-forming principles of behavioral psychology to design a way for students to get personalized help with their hardest classes – the moment they feel overwhelmed. The creation of a two-sided marketplace for peer-to-peer tutoring would empower students to efficiently learn, work, and achieve their goals.

How I Built This with Kevin Huang

I first sketched various possible features that could empower students. My initial hypothesis was that students couldn’t find tutors that matched their learning style and personality. My plan was to survey learning preferences during onboarding to develop an algorithm that shows students the right tutor for them.

I realized my initial designs were not going to work because it didn’t address the main struggles that most students faced – getting stuck on a confusing problem or missing a class. These needs are more frequent and pressing than compatibility between students and tutors. Prioritizing reliability over compatability also leads to a more viable and sustainable product.
Proressive iterations of the time picker enhanced by testing with users.
prototype

Making it dribbble-worthy

I tested my prototype by asking students to complete certain tasks within the app. After closely observing students interacting with the app, I enhanced the user flow and information architecture to optimize usability.

I created a system of components to design a cohesive visual identity. Understanding that users may be in a stressed and agitated state, I used soft elements and round forms to convey a reassuring and calming effect. The choice of green as the primary color was inspired by its association with nature and growth, and by stories of its use in hospitals to promote stress-relief and healing. I designed a set of custom illustrations and icons that represent streamlined learning. I also explored designing a dark mode because students often study through the night before exams.
“It’s like Uber, but for...”
Students want to feel successful and get good grades. Tutors want to make both an impact and money.

To provide problem help on-demand in a convenient way for both parties, I drew inspiration from my DoorDash experience. Tutors can log on or off whenever they want. When a request is made, they have two minutes to confirm they can solve it. This system effectively develops new market segments; potential customers who didn't need a full hour lesson are more willing to get help, and tutors are motivated to compete by confirming in a timely manner and being available at the right time.

You're the boss
Power users who want more can see a tutor availabilitys and schedule a private lesson. Tutor enjoy the flexibility of choosing when, where, and how much they earn.

How might I design the best time picker for students to see availabilities and schedule a private lesson? Testing my designs with tutors, I learned most students schedule 1-hour lessons. I designed the time picker to help users efficiently browse dates.
Easy to get started
Students simply select the class they want help with and available tutors are shown. Privacy comes first, so everyone is on a first-name basis.

How might I develop the market, boost conversion rate, and encourage customer retention?
Students experience the value proposition before creating an account. Collaborating with popular USC students leverages micro-influencers marketing to dismantle stigma, increase exchange value through social rewards, and reframe tutoring as "something someone like me does." By alleviating the pain of frustrating concepts for students, opening Accel becomes a conditioned response that drives customer retention.
